scenario: UTI with dysuria and frequency in adult woman
category: Acute
density: Sparse
---

Doctor: Good afternoon, Ms. Jennings. What brings you in?

Patient: I think I have a bladder infection. It burns when I pee and I feel like I have to go constantly, even when barely anything comes out.

Doctor: When did this start?

Patient: Day before yesterday. It's gotten worse since then.

Doctor: Any blood in your urine?

Patient: Maybe a little pinkish this morning. That's what made me call.

Doctor: Any back pain, fever, nausea?

Patient: No, none of that. Just the burning and the urgency.

Doctor: Have you had UTIs before?

Patient: One about two years ago. Same thing — they gave me antibiotics and it cleared right up.

Doctor: Okay. We had you give a urine sample when you checked in. The dipstick shows positive leukocyte esterase, positive nitrites, and trace blood — all consistent with a UTI. I'm going to send it for culture too, but I'm comfortable starting antibiotics now.

Patient: Oh good. What are you giving me?

Doctor: Nitrofurantoin — brand name Macrobid — 100 milligrams twice a day for five days. Take it with food, it absorbs better that way.

Patient: Any allergies I should worry about? I don't have any that I know of.

Doctor: You should be fine. Most common side effect is a little nausea, which taking it with food helps. Drink plenty of water to flush things out. You should feel significantly better within a day or two.

Patient: And if I don't?

Doctor: If your symptoms aren't improving within 48 hours, or if you develop fever, back pain, or vomiting — call us right away because that could mean the infection has moved to the kidneys. Otherwise, finish the full course and you should be good.

Patient: Perfect. Thank you.

Doctor: You're welcome. The prescription's heading to your pharmacy now. Feel better.
