So I saw Lily Thompson today — she's a four-year-old girl coming in for her four-year well-child visit. She's a new patient, transferred over from Dr. Parekh's office, and I did have her records available prior to the visit. Mom, Danielle, was present throughout.

So starting with concerns — mom's main concern is picky eating. Lily basically subsists on chicken nuggets, mac and cheese, plain pasta, and grilled cheese. Mom has difficulty getting her to eat vegetables. We'll come back to that. Otherwise, mom says she's been doing really well overall.

Socially, Lily attends preschool four mornings a week, loves it, plays well with other kids. Her teacher reports she's social and is starting to recognize some letters. She's in swim lessons on Saturdays and just started a dance class.

Going through developmental milestones — language is excellent. She gave me her full name, spoke in full sentences, clear articulation, uses pronouns correctly, can talk about things not present. She was counting, identified colors — mom says she's had colors since two and a half. She draws people with a head, body, arms, legs, and sometimes fingers, no neck yet, which is actually very good for four. She can dress herself mostly independently — manages shirts, pants, and shoes, though she gets them on the wrong feet sometimes — needs help with buttons and zippers, which is normal.

Potty training — fully trained during the day. She has occasional nighttime accidents, about once a week, and they're still using a pull-up at bedtime. I reassured mom that nighttime wetting is completely normal at four and we don't consider it an issue until age six or seven.

Dental hygiene — she brushes her teeth with mom going back over afterward, which is appropriate since kids don't have the dexterity to brush well until around six or seven. She's seeing a dentist every six months and has no cavities.

Behaviorally, she can have meltdowns when tired or when she doesn't get her way, but mom says it's significantly improved from age three. She's now negotiating rather than just melting down, which I noted is actually a sign of emotional development. She's able to verbalize when she's mad, which mom has been working on teaching her. Fears include the dark — managed with a nightlight — and she went through a phase of being afraid of dogs, which has resolved.

Sleep — bath at seven-fifteen, books at seven-thirty, lights out by eight, falls asleep within fifteen to twenty minutes, wakes around six-thirty. So approximately ten and a half hours, which is right in the ideal range for her age. She naps inconsistently — maybe twice a week at school but not at home on weekends. I told mom that nap dropping is normal at four.

Back to the eating — typical day is Cheerios or a waffle with sometimes a banana for breakfast, lunch at school she reportedly eats okay, dinner is hit or miss, maybe a third of the time she'll eat what the family eats. Drinks whole milk and water, juice occasionally about twice a week. I counseled mom to switch from whole milk to two percent now that Lily is four. For the picky eating, I counseled on the division of responsibility approach — keep offering foods without pressure, put small amounts of vegetables on her plate alongside preferred foods without negotiating or rewarding, as it can take ten to fifteen exposures before a child will try a new food. I also recommended involving Lily in food prep and stopping the practice of making separate meals — serve one family meal with one known accepted food alongside, and she won't starve. Mom was receptive.

Growth measurements — height is thirty-nine point two inches, forty-fifth percentile. Weight is thirty-five point six pounds, fifty-fifth percentile. BMI is at the fifty-eighth percentile. All tracking consistently on her curve and consistent with prior measurements.

On physical exam — she was cooperative and interactive throughout. Oropharynx: tonsils normal, teeth look good, no visible cavities. Ears: bilateral tympanic membranes normal. Eyes: pupils equal, red reflex normal bilaterally, extraocular movements intact. Vision screen done in the hall — passed at twenty-twenty-five in both eyes bilaterally, which is normal for age. Hearing screen — passed. Cardiovascular: regular rate and rhythm, no murmur. Lungs: clear. Abdomen: soft, non-tender. Musculoskeletal: normal alignment of legs and hips, full range of motion, spine straight, no curvature. Skin: clear.

Gross motor assessment — she hopped on one foot for three to four to five hops, caught a ball, and held a single-leg stance for approximately four seconds, which is appropriate for age. All gross motor milestones on track.

I did counsel mom on family history of refractive error — both parents wear glasses — and I let her know that while Lily may eventually need glasses, her vision is normal at this time.

Immunizations — she received her four-year vaccines today: DTaP, IPV, MMR, and varicella, given as three injections total. She received her flu vaccine in October of this season so she is covered there. I let mom know the next big vaccine round won't be until eleven or twelve.

Anticipatory guidance covered: car seat — she's in a forward-facing Graco with a five-point harness, I advised mom to check the weight and height limits on the label and to keep her in the five-point harness as long as she fits rather than rushing to a booster. Water safety — despite swim lessons, I emphasized direct supervision around water at all times without exception. Screen time — she watches about an hour of TV after school and about thirty minutes of iPad on weekends, sometimes more. I noted the guideline is about an hour a day of high-quality content and that she's right in range, and reinforced no screens close to bedtime, which they're already doing with their bath and books routine.

Overall, Lily is doing great. Development is right on track across language, motor, social, and cognitive domains. Mom was reassured. Plan is to return for five-year well-child visit unless needed sooner.
